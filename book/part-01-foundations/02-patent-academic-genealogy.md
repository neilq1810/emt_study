Chapter 1 told the story; this chapter traces the **intellectual property and
academic lineages** that structured it. Electromagnetic tracking's commercial
shape was determined as much by patents as by physics: the AC vs. pulsed-DC fork
(Ch. 8) maps onto two distinct patent lineages, and the medical industry rests on
a third. We follow the priority chains from the foundational patents to the
modern families, then the academic genealogy from the 1979 formalization to
today's research groups, and close with how IP shaped architecture.

---

## 2.1 The foundational patents and the AC/pulsed-DC fork

Three patents anchor the field, one per major lineage:

### Kuipers (1975) — the Polhemus/AC lineage
**Jack Kuipers, US 3,868,565 (issued 1975), "Object tracking and orientation
determination means, system and process"** [@kuipers1975] is the foundational
magnetic position/orientation patent and the IP root of the Polhemus lineage
(Ch. 1 §1.4, Ch. 28.1). A historically important nuance that **resolves the Ch. 1
"SPASYN" open question**: the patent describes a **nutating** magnetic field
generated by supplying *a DC signal to one coil and an AC-modulated carrier to
others* of an orthogonal coil set, sensed in orthogonal directions at the object
[@kuipers1975]. So the very origin of the field is **not** a clean "AC" system —
it is a nutating/mixed-excitation scheme; the *continuous-AC quasi-static dipole*
formulation that defines the modern AC lineage was the **1979 Raab et al.**
academic formalization [@raab1979] (§2.4), built on this patent base. A companion
Kuipers patent (US 3,983,474, 1976) covers the coordinate-transformation method.
(conf: high — patent text verified.)

### Blood (1990) — the Ascension/pulsed-DC lineage
**Ernest B. Blood, US 4,945,305 (1990), assigned to Ascension Technology**,
"Device for quantitatively measuring the relative position and orientation of two
bodies **in the presence of metals utilizing direct current magnetic fields**"
[@blood1990] is the foundational **pulsed-DC** patent (Ch. 1 §1.6, Ch. 6 §6.4,
Ch. 28.2). Its title states the value proposition directly — pose measurement
*in the presence of metals* — i.e. the conductive-distortion immunity that
motivates the DC approach (Ch. 6). Blood, a co-author of Raab et al. 1979, thus
appears in **both** lineages: the AC academic formalization and the pulsed-DC
patent fork. A later Blood patent (WO 1996/002008) addresses **non-dipole** field
modeling for larger generators (Ch. 7 §7.2). (conf: high — verified.)

### Ben-Haim / Acker (1990s) — the Biosense/medical lineage
**Shlomo Ben-Haim, US 5,391,199**, describes catheter localization: a sensor coil
in the catheter responds to three external radiator coils, whose signal amplitudes
compute the sensor location [@benhaim1995] — the IP basis of **CARTO** (Ch. 1
§1.8, Ch. 28.4). It sits in a broad Biosense family (including **Acker et al.
US 5,558,091**, "Magnetic determination of position and orientation," and numerous
follow-ons). This lineage adapts the AC magnetic-coupling principle specifically
to the *medical, single-small-sensor, large-external-generator* topology
(reciprocity, Ch. 5 §5.5). (conf: high — verified patent numbers.)

## 2.2 Patent genealogy: priority chains and claim evolution

The structure of the IP landscape (conf: med — based on the verified anchors and
the cited follow-on families; a full claim-chart analysis is out of scope):

```
Kuipers US3,868,565 (1975, nutating field) ──► US3,983,474 (1976, coord. transform)
        │ (Polhemus / AC lineage)
        ├─► Raab et al. 1979 (academic AC quasi-static dipole formalization)
        └─► later AC tracking & distortion-compensation patents (Ch. 27)

Blood US4,945,305 (1990, DC in presence of metals) ──► WO96/002008 (non-dipole)
        │ (Ascension / pulsed-DC lineage)
        └─► trakSTAR/driveBAY-era DC patents (→ Roper/NDI, Ch. 28.2)

Ben-Haim US5,391,199 + Acker US5,558,091 (catheter localization)
        │ (Biosense / medical lineage)
        └─► CARTO patent family (6,690,963; 6,484,118; 6,239,724; …) + distortion/
            metal-immunity patents (e.g. US7,321,228) (Ch. 27)
```

Two genealogical observations:
- **Claims migrate from "how to measure pose" to "how to survive distortion."**
  The early patents claim the core measurement; the dense later patent activity
  (Ch. 27's witness-sensor, dynamic-metal-immunity, and compensation patents)
  claims *robustness* — mirroring the field's shift from feasibility to
  reliability (Ch. 30).
- **Priority vs. publication dates differ**; the dates above are issue dates.
  Filing/priority dates (earlier) determine actual precedence and are the correct
  basis for any precedence claim — flagged for the *Open questions*.

## 2.3 The AC vs. pulsed-DC patent landscape

The two excitation lineages are not merely technical alternatives (Ch. 6, 8) but
distinct **patent estates** that shaped competition:

| | AC lineage | Pulsed-DC lineage |
|---|---|---|
| Root patent | Kuipers US3,868,565 [@kuipers1975] | Blood US4,945,305 [@blood1990] |
| Company | Polhemus | Ascension |
| Claimed advantage | continuous high-rate 6-DOF | metal/eddy immunity |
| Academic anchor | Raab et al. 1979 [@raab1979] | (Blood, co-author, applied to DC) |

This bifurcation gave the two Vermont companies (Ch. 28) defensible, largely
non-overlapping positions for years, which is part of why *both* AC and pulsed-DC
survived commercially rather than one displacing the other.

## 2.4 Academic genealogy

The academic lineage is anchored by **Raab, Blood, Steiner & Jones (1979)**
[@raab1979], the most-cited EMT paper and the formal statement of the
quasi-static magnetic-dipole 6-DOF method (Ch. 1 §1.5, Ch. 5). Its descendants
form recognizable branches:

- **Estimation/algorithm branch** — the predictor/corrector idea (Ch. 11/21/23)
  flowing into modern recursive estimators [@kalman1960; @julier2004;
  @barshalom2001].
- **Excitation/decoding branch** — alternative excitations: rotating fields and
  transmitter arrays (Paperno; Plotkin & Paperno) [@paperno2001; @plotkin2003].
- **Medical validation branch** — the assessment/validation school (Birkfellner,
  Hummel, Yaniv, Cleary, Peters, Maier-Hein, Franz, Haidegger)
  [@birkfellner1998; @hummel2005; @yaniv2009; @franz2014], which produced the
  standardized protocols (Ch. 26) and the definitive review.
- **Calibration/compensation branch** — Kindratenko's survey and NN calibration,
  and the witness-sensor work (Cavaliere & Cantillon-Murphy)
  [@kindratenko2000; @kindratenko2005; @cavaliere2023].
- **Clinical branch** — Gepstein & Ben-Haim's electroanatomical mapping
  [@gepstein1997] seeding the entire EP application area (Ch. 29).

These branches are not isolated: the medical-validation and calibration schools
cite back to Raab and forward into the commercial systems they assess (Ch. 28),
making the academic and commercial genealogies tightly interwoven.

## 2.5 How IP shaped commercial architecture

The causal arrows from IP to product:

- **The AC/DC patent split institutionalized the two architectures** (§2.3) and,
  with them, the two error-profile trade-offs (Ch. 6) that persist in products
  today (Ch. 28).
- **The medical-localization patents (Ben-Haim/Acker) enabled a closed, vertically
  integrated clinical ecosystem** (CARTO under J&J, Ch. 28.4) distinct from the
  general-purpose OEM trackers of the AC/DC lineages.
- **Consolidation followed the IP**: NDI's acquisition of Ascension (2012, Ch. 28.2)
  brought the pulsed-DC estate under the same roof as NDI's medical EM business,
  concentrating the OEM medical-tracking IP.
- **The locus of innovation moved to robustness IP** (§2.2): the patent frontier is
  now distortion compensation and fusion (Ch. 27, 30), matching the open problems.

> **Takeaway.** EMT's three foundational patents — Kuipers (AC lineage, via a
> nutating field), Blood (pulsed-DC, metal immunity), and Ben-Haim/Acker (medical
> catheter localization) — plus the 1979 Raab academic formalization, explain not
> just *who* built EMT but *why* the products take the shapes they do. The patent
> and academic genealogies are two views of the same family tree.
