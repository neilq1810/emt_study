An EMT front end lives in one of the electromagnetically dirtiest environments
imaginable — an operating room or cath lab full of switching supplies,
electrosurgical units, fluoroscopy, motors, and mains wiring — while trying to
resolve sub-µT signals (Ch. 4 §4.7). This chapter covers the defensive
engineering that makes that possible: **filtering** (anti-alias and band-select),
**shielding/grounding/guarding** (with the crucial caveat that you must *not*
magnetically shield the signal you came to measure), and the **EMC and electrical-
safety** obligations that medical use imposes via IEC 60601 [@iec60601_1;
@iec60601_1_2].

## 17.1 Anti-alias and band-select filtering

Two distinct filtering jobs, often confused:

- **Band-select (the EMT advantage).** The signal sits at the *known* excitation
  frequency (AC) or in a known post-step window (pulsed-DC). A bandpass around
  the excitation band rejects the enormous out-of-band interference —
  geomagnetic DC, mains 50/60 Hz and harmonics, drift — *before* it can consume
  AFE dynamic range or fold into the signal. This is the analog precursor to the
  synchronous detection of Ch. 20, and it is why EMT can work despite signals far
  below the geomagnetic field: it never tries to measure absolute field, only a
  narrow band.
- **Anti-alias (mandatory before sampling).** Any energy above the ADC Nyquist
  frequency aliases into the baseband and is indistinguishable from signal
  (Ch. 18). A fixed anti-alias filter must attenuate everything above $f_s/2$ to
  below the noise floor. **Σ-Δ ADCs** (Ch. 18) relax this by oversampling — the
  anti-alias filter only needs to guard the much higher modulator rate — which is
  a major reason Σ-Δ is popular in EMT.

Filter **group delay** is a latency term (Ch. 12 eq. 12.1) and a phase term that
the synchronous detector must account for (a frequency-dependent phase shift
becomes a calibration constant, Ch. 26). Linear-phase designs simplify this.

## 17.2 Shielding, grounding, and guarding

### The cardinal rule: shield E, not B
The instinct to "shield the sensitive coil" is dangerous in EMT. A
**ferromagnetic (mu-metal) magnetic shield would attenuate the very magnetic
signal** the system depends on, and a thick conductive shield would host its own
eddy currents (Ch. 6) and distort the field. So:

- **Electrostatic (Faraday) shielding** *is* used — a thin, slotted/gapped
  conductive shield around the coil and leads blocks **capacitively coupled
  electric-field interference** while (if properly slotted to prevent a shorted
  turn) passing the magnetic signal. The slot prevents the shield from acting as
  a closed eddy-current loop.
- **Magnetic shielding is generally avoided** in the working volume; interference
  at the *magnetic* level is instead rejected by band-selection and synchronous
  detection (frequency-domain separation) rather than by physical shielding.

### Grounding and guarding
- **Single-point / star grounding** and careful separation of analog, digital,
  and drive returns prevent ground-loop currents from injecting interference into
  the nV-class signal path.
- **Guarding** (driving a shield/trace to the input common-mode potential)
  minimizes leakage and effective cable capacitance around high-impedance coil
  inputs (Ch. 16 §16.3), preserving bandwidth and CMRR.
- **Cable discipline.** Twisted, shielded, mechanically stable leads reduce
  loop-area pickup and **triboelectric** noise (charge generated by cable
  flexing) — a genuine error source for moving catheter sensors (Ch. 14.2,
  Part IX). Keep loop area small and the cable still where possible.

### Separating the transmit and receive domains
The generator drive is a large signal at the same frequency as the µT-level
receive signal. Crosstalk from drive into receive (through shared grounds, power,
or radiated coupling) directly corrupts the measurement; physical separation,
independent supplies/returns, and careful reference distribution (Ch. 10) are
required.

## 17.3 EMC and the medical-grade obligation

### Electrical safety: IEC 60601-1
A tracker with a **patient-applied part** (a catheter or skin-contact sensor) is
governed by IEC 60601-1 [@iec60601_1]. The constraints that shape the AFE/power
design:

- **Means of Protection (MOP).** Two independent means of protection against
  electric shock are required between mains and the patient — realized as
  reinforced/double insulation and/or protective earth, with defined isolation
  voltages.
- **Patient leakage current limits.** These are stringent and depend on the
  **applied-part classification**. A catheter sensor in or near the heart is a
  **Type CF** applied part, with patient leakage limits on the order of
  **10 µA (normal condition) / 50 µA (single-fault)** — far tighter than Type B/BF
  (≈100 µA / 500 µA) (conf: high — limits per IEC 60601-1 as reported by multiple
  compliance sources). For an intracardiac EMT sensor (Ch. 1 §1.8, EP) the CF
  limits are the binding constraint and force **galvanic isolation** of the
  sensor channel.
- **Isolation barriers** between the patient-connected front end and the rest of
  the system (isolated amplifiers, isolated power, digital isolators) — which add
  their own noise and bandwidth constraints the AFE designer must budget (Ch. 16).

### Electromagnetic compatibility: IEC 60601-1-2
The EMC collateral standard [@iec60601_1_2] governs both **emissions** (the
tracker must not disturb other devices) and **immunity** (it must maintain
**essential performance** amid disturbances). The **4th edition (2014)** moved to
a **risk- and environment-based** philosophy: the manufacturer defines the use
environment (professional healthcare facility / home / special) and the
*essential performance* (here, pose accuracy/availability within stated bounds),
then demonstrates immunity to the relevant phenomena (radiated/conducted RF, ESD,
bursts, surges, magnetic fields) at levels set for that environment. **Edition 4.1
(Amendment 1, 2020)** refined the risk-management and test requirements. (conf:
high — per IEC and compliance literature.)

The EMT-specific implication: **"essential performance" must be defined in pose
terms** — e.g. "position error remains within X mm or the system flags loss of
tracking" — and the immunity test plan must exercise the realistic threats
(electrosurgery, nearby imaging). This couples directly to the error budget
(Ch. 25), the fault/flagging behavior of the solver (Ch. 12 §12.4, Ch. 24), and
the clinical validation of Ch. 29.

### Power systems
Medical-grade power adds: isolated, low-noise supplies (switching-supply noise
must not reach the nV signal path — hence shielding, filtering, and often linear
post-regulation for the analog rails), defined isolation/leakage per
IEC 60601-1, and clean, stable references for the DDS/ADC clocks (Ch. 10) since
supply noise modulates both excitation and conversion.

## 17.4 Part V synthesis
Parts IV–V together define the *receive chain* from field to digital samples:
the sensor sets the noise floor (Ch. 15), the LNA/in-amp must preserve it while
spanning ~100 dB of dynamic range (Ch. 16), and filtering/shielding/EMC/power
(this chapter) keep the dirty environment and safety constraints from spoiling
either — all while *not* shielding away the magnetic signal. The output is a
clean, isolated, band-limited, well-scaled signal ready for conversion (Ch. 18)
and synchronous detection (Ch. 20).
